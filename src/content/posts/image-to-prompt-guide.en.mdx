Image to prompt is the process of converting an image back into the kind of text prompt that could have created it. Instead of guessing which words produce a certain style, you upload the image and let an AI model write the prompt for you — describing the subject, composition, lighting, color palette, and artistic style in a form that AI image generators understand.

If you have ever seen an image and thought "how do I make something like this in Midjourney?", image to prompt is the answer.

## What Is Image to Prompt?

AI image generators like Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, and Flux turn text into images. Image to prompt runs that process in reverse: it takes an image as input and produces a text prompt as output. The result is sometimes called a "reverse prompt" or "prompt extraction."

A good image to prompt tool does more than list objects. It captures:

- The subject and what it is doing
- Composition and framing (close-up, wide shot, rule of thirds)
- Lighting (golden hour, studio softbox, neon rim light)
- Color palette and mood
- Artistic style (cinematic, watercolor, 3D render, anime)
- Camera and lens language (85mm portrait, shallow depth of field)

These are exactly the details that make AI-generated images look the way they do — and the details most people struggle to write from scratch.

## Why Reverse-Engineer Prompts?

The most common use case is recreating a style. You find an image with a look you love — a product photo, an illustration, a cinematic portrait — and you want more images in that style, but with your own subject. Image to prompt gives you the style recipe; you swap in your subject and generate.

It is also one of the fastest ways to learn prompt engineering. Reading prompts that describe real images teaches you which words actually matter: lighting terms, lens terms, style keywords, and composition language. After a dozen extractions, you start writing noticeably better prompts yourself.

Other practical uses include:

- Building a consistent visual style across a brand or content series
- Recreating an AI image when you lost the original prompt
- Adapting a style from one generator to another (e.g. Midjourney → Flux)
- Creating prompt variations for A/B testing visuals in marketing

## How Image to Prompt Works

Modern image to prompt tools use multimodal AI models — models that can "see" images and reason about them in language. When you upload an image, the model analyzes its visual content and writes a structured description tuned for image generation.

The instruction matters as much as the model. A general instruction produces a general prompt, while a Midjourney-specific instruction produces output with Midjourney conventions — aspect ratio flags, stylize parameters, and the concise keyword-heavy phrasing Midjourney responds to best.

That is why dedicated presets exist: the same image yields a different prompt depending on which generator you plan to feed it to.

## General vs Midjourney vs Stable Diffusion vs Flux Prompts

Each generator has its own prompting dialect:

- **General prompts** are natural-language descriptions that work across models. They prioritize clarity: subject, setting, style, lighting, mood.
- **Midjourney prompts** are compact and keyword-dense. They often include parameters like `--ar 16:9` for aspect ratio and `--s` for stylization, and they reward strong style adjectives over full sentences.
- **Stable Diffusion prompts** lean on comma-separated tags and quality tokens ("masterpiece, best quality, detailed"), and they pair well with negative prompts to exclude artifacts.
- **Flux prompts** favor rich, descriptive natural language. Flux follows longer sentences more faithfully than earlier models, so detail pays off.

If you are not sure which to use, start with the general prompt, then switch presets to compare — the differences teach you a lot about each generator.

## Tips for Better Results

- **Use clean source images.** The clearer the subject and style, the more precise the extracted prompt.
- **Crop out distractions.** Watermarks, borders, and UI elements can leak into the prompt.
- **Treat the output as a draft.** Replace the subject, adjust the aspect ratio, and remove details you do not want before generating.
- **Compare presets.** Running the same image through two presets shows you which style cues each generator cares about.
- **Combine with OCR when needed.** If your image contains text you also need, the [extract text from picture](/extract-text-from-picture) tool pulls it out while preserving layout.
